From the Third to the Sixth Axis: A CNC Journey

Welcome to the documentation of my journey through the axes in CNC!

The Beginning

In the summer of 2021, the global shipping crisis had reached the point at which it was almost completely unviable to import furniture frames from the far east. Since this was our main method of acquiring complicated frames, we faced a bit of a problem, but also an unlikely opportunity.

For the first time in many decades, there was an opportunity to bring some manufacturing back to the UK, as the financial element suddenly made sense in the face of the enormous shipping costs. It was this realisation (coupled with a vision and optimism regarding what this could bring to the business) that led to the beginning of our search for a suitable machine.

Many suitors presented themselves, claiming to be the machine manufacturers who could solve our problems, but most came up short - in one way or another. It was only after having heard out the big players in the game that a previously unknown to us company, Bacci, entered the fray. Their demonstrations were impressive, and the machine had one thing the others didn’t: a sixth axis.

Once the paperwork was finished, we began the long wait for delivery. The day finally arrived in mid-July 2022. Three weeks of installation and training ensued and then we were left alone; the theory was there, but the specifics and knowledge needed for practicality still eluded us.

As is apt to happen, other projects then took the front seat. We were, however, provided some relief from our "frame-woes" by a deal struck with our big customer to purchase an enormous number at once. One thing after another meant that, by the end of 2022, we had still only cut theory-proving pieces on the machine.

Now, at the beginning of 2023, the 6-axis project is back on, and we are approaching it with a renewed vigour. I hope in the posts below to be able to bring you along on this journey, as it happens, to give a flavour of the difficulties we face in bringing our vision to fruition.

Picking Back Up

24/01/2023

Last week I finished an advanced course with our CAM software provider and with this training fresh in my mind, I feel a renewed sense of enthusiasm. The software in question is called Alphacam, and it’s our sole port of call for the toolpath generation that we need to create the machine programs. The numeric code (NC) generated from this application is then fed into the proprietary software that was shipped with the product – Pitagora. I can use that to do the final checks on my program and turn it into machine code that that can be run.

All very well in principle, but - unfortunately - not so straight forward in practice. But okay, let’s try to build on the things I learnt last week – at least it's somewhere to start.

The training was specifically focused on utilising the advanced 5-axis tools that are available within Alphacam: these don’t ship as standard with the package and we’ve had to shell out quite a bit extra to acquire them, but it will 100% be worth it for what we’re trying to achieve. However, I just want to start off small - prove some concepts first… I’m going to need something to cut.

So, my first port of call is our CAD software, SolidWorks, where I can quickly design something made specifically for the purpose of testing. I whip up a funny little model that has specific features I can target with different machining methods within Alphacam – I creatively call it: “AlphaTestPiece”.

It’s not much to look at, but I reckon that this will be a great project to get me off the ground – it has a bit of everything. So, I have my subject and now it’s time to start trying to bring it to life. I switch over to Alphacam and begin attempting to create some tool paths. It’s worth noting that I designed AlphaTestPiece with our 3-axis CNC in mind – a machine we understand very well. I can layer up bits of CNC-machined plywood and get a “blank” of a known starting shape, which is essential and easy to do with our existing knowledge.

James Hearn - 2023
